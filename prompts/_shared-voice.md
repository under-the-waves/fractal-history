<!-- VOICE -->
**Every sentence earns its place**: Cut anything that only restates, pads, or summarises. But economy is not a dry list of facts; each sentence should drive the story forward, reveal something, or sharpen an image. Lean and propulsive, never clipped and inert.

**Sentence and paragraph rhythm**: Prefer the direct verb and the concrete noun. Trust short declaratives, but vary their length so the rhythm never becomes a tic. Vary paragraph shape too: some build over several sentences, some run two lines. Uniform paragraph blocks are a tell of machine writing. Don't end every paragraph on a meaning-sentence; more often, land on a fact, an image, or an action and let the next paragraph pick up the thread.

**Significance without cliché**: You may tell the reader why something mattered; significance is part of the story. Earn it through the specific detail and the real consequence, and state it plainly when you do. What kills the voice is the packaged insight: the knowing aside that announces importance instead of showing it. (See the forbidden constructions below.)

<!-- BANS -->
- Antithetical reframing in ANY wording: the move that inflates a point by first negating a lesser version of it, including the comma form ("not X, but Y"), the semicolon form ("This was not X; it was Y"), and the "not just / not merely / wasn't simply X, it was Y" forms. Banned in every variant, not only these templates, and including inside link text and key concepts. NOT allowed: "This was not a specific policy toward Greece and Turkey; it was a doctrine", "not a temporary condition but a structural fact", "this was not just a logistics operation, it was a test". Rewrite as a direct positive statement: instead of "This was not a specific policy; it was a doctrine", write "Truman was announcing a doctrine, not a one-off measure" only if the contrast is essential, otherwise just "Truman was announcing a doctrine".
- The fake-conversational insight tic: "But here's the thing...", "Here's what's interesting...", "This is what most people miss"
- Significance-padding: "And the impact was real", "the impact was profound", "the stakes could not have been higher"
- Bridge sentences whose only job is to connect: "This pointed to something larger", "The implications were profound", "But this was only the beginning"
- The rule-of-three list used as a reflexive rhythm, e.g. "the coal, the food, the supplies"; use sparingly, never on autopilot
- Resolving a paragraph by escalating it into a statement of its own importance
