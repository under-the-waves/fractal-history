# Verified fact base — Emergence of Life on Earth (1A-E8F2G)

This is the **grading ground truth** for the marking-engine prototype. The marker is told
to treat statements consistent with these facts as correct, statements that contradict them
as factual errors, and statements within the "Disputed / open" notes as defensible
interpretation (NOT errors).

The five sub-anchors below ARE the coverage rubric: a learner narrative should touch all five.

Scope: the origin and early evolution of life on Earth, from first self-replicating
molecules to the first complex multicellular organisms. ~4 billion years ago (Gya) to
~600 million years ago (Mya).

---

## Sub-anchor 1 — Origin of Self-Replicating Molecules

Verified facts:
- Earth formed ~4.54 Gya; the earliest widely accepted evidence of life is ~3.5 Gya, with
  contested chemical traces pushing toward ~3.7–4.0 Gya.
- The Miller–Urey experiment (Stanley Miller, working under Harold Urey at the **University
  of Chicago**, 1952) sparked a flask of water, methane, ammonia and hydrogen and produced
  amino acids. It demonstrated that organic building blocks can form abiotically; it did NOT
  create life.
- A central transition was the appearance of a molecule that could template copies of itself,
  enabling heredity, variation, and therefore natural selection.
- The leading hypothesis is the **RNA world**: RNA can both store information and catalyse
  reactions (ribozymes), so it could act as both gene and enzyme before DNA and proteins
  divided those roles.
- Copying with errors (mutation) plus differential survival is what turns chemistry into
  biology.

Disputed / open (defensible, do not mark as error):
- Whether life began with replication first (RNA world) or **metabolism first** (self-sustaining
  chemical cycles at hydrothermal vents) is genuinely debated.
- The setting — warm little pond, hydrothermal vents, deep crust, even extraterrestrial
  delivery of organics (panspermia of building blocks) — is unsettled.
- The exact early atmosphere composition (how reducing it was) is debated, which bears on how
  readily Miller–Urey-type chemistry ran.

## Sub-anchor 2 — Evolution of Photosynthesis

Verified facts:
- For roughly the first billion-plus years, life was single-celled and lacked a nucleus
  (prokaryotes); early metabolisms ran on chemical energy.
- **Cyanobacteria** evolved **oxygenic photosynthesis**: using sunlight to convert carbon
  dioxide and water into sugars, releasing oxygen as a waste product.
- Oxygenic photosynthesis gave life an effectively unlimited energy source (sunlight) and is
  the source of nearly all the free oxygen on Earth.

Disputed / open:
- Photosynthesis is old; earlier **anoxygenic** photosynthesis (not producing oxygen) preceded
  the oxygenic form. The precise date oxygenic photosynthesis first evolved (well before the
  oxygen actually accumulated) is debated, with estimates spanning ~3.0–2.4 Gya and earlier.

## Sub-anchor 3 — Great Oxidation Event (GOE)

Verified facts:
- The **Great Oxidation Event** was the rise of free oxygen in the oceans and atmosphere
  **~2.4 billion years ago** (commonly ~2.4–2.3 Gya).
- Oxygen first reacted with dissolved iron in the oceans, precipitating it as **banded iron
  formations** (the banded rocks still mined today), before free oxygen accumulated in the air.
- To most existing anaerobic life, oxygen was toxic; the GOE likely caused a mass die-off of
  anaerobes — sometimes called the "oxygen catastrophe."
- The GOE is associated with severe glaciation (the Huronian glaciation), plausibly because
  oxygen reacted with methane (a strong greenhouse gas), cooling the planet.

Disputed / open:
- "Whiffs" of oxygen may have appeared locally before the main event; the GOE's timing,
  abruptness, and whether it was a single step or staged are debated.
- The causal link between the GOE and the Huronian glaciation is plausible but not settled.

## Sub-anchor 4 — Endosymbiosis and Eukaryotic Cells

Verified facts:
- **Eukaryotes** (cells with a nucleus and internal compartments) arose later and are larger
  and more energy-rich than prokaryotes.
- **Endosymbiotic theory** (associated with Lynn Margulis): a host cell engulfed an
  oxygen-using bacterium and retained it rather than digesting it; that endosymbiont became
  the **mitochondrion**, the cell's energy organelle.
- A separate endosymbiosis of a **cyanobacterium** gave rise to **chloroplasts** in the
  lineage leading to algae and plants.
- Key evidence: mitochondria and chloroplasts retain their own DNA and replicate
  semi-independently, a relic of their free-living bacterial ancestry.

Disputed / open:
- The host that acquired the mitochondrion (an archaeon, and which lineage) and the order of
  events (nucleus first vs mitochondrion first) are actively researched.

## Sub-anchor 5 — First Multicellular Organisms

Verified facts:
- Greater energy availability in eukaryotes enabled cells to stay together, adhere, signal,
  and **specialise** (division of labour between cell types).
- Multicellularity evolved independently multiple times across the tree of life.
- The **Ediacaran biota** (~635–541 Mya, conventionally cited around ~600 Mya) are the
  earliest large, soft-bodied multicellular organisms in the fossil record — quilted,
  frondlike forms lacking mouths, eyes, guts, or skeletons.
- These preceded the **Cambrian explosion** (~541 Mya), when animals with eyes, guts, hard
  shells and mobility diversified rapidly.

Disputed / open:
- The biological affinities of many Ediacaran organisms (animals? a separate failed
  experiment? something else) are genuinely unresolved.
- Whether multicellularity in this lineage was driven primarily by oxygen levels, genetics,
  or ecology is debated.

## Common misconceptions the marker should catch

- Miller–Urey "created life" (it created building blocks, not life).
- Evolution / natural selection "started" before self-replication existed (selection requires
  heritable replication).
- Oxygen was always beneficial to early life (it was toxic to the anaerobes of the time).
- Humans / animals appear in this period (this period ends before the Cambrian; no animals
  with eyes/guts/shells yet).
- Mitochondria came from photosynthetic bacteria (those became chloroplasts; mitochondria
  came from oxygen-using non-photosynthetic bacteria).
- Lamarckian framing ("organisms developed oxygen tolerance because they needed it").
