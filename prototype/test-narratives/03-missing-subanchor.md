Life on Earth started with chemistry. In the 1950s the Miller–Urey experiment at the
University of Chicago showed that amino acids, the building blocks of proteins, could form from
nothing but gas, water and electric sparks. It didn't make life, but it made the ingredients.
The real threshold came when some molecule, probably RNA, first managed to copy itself. RNA is
useful because it can both carry information and speed up reactions, so it could be gene and
enzyme at once. Once copying existed, copying mistakes existed too, and the variants that
copied best won out. That is where natural selection begins.

For more than a billion years life stayed tiny and simple, single cells without a nucleus.
Then cyanobacteria invented photosynthesis, using sunlight to turn carbon dioxide and water
into sugar. This gave life an almost unlimited energy source, because the sun never runs out,
and cyanobacteria bloomed across the shallow seas.

Eventually one larger cell engulfed a smaller bacterium and kept it alive inside instead of
digesting it. That permanent partnership became the mitochondria that power our cells today. We
know this because mitochondria still carry their own DNA. The same thing happened separately
with a photosynthetic bacterium, which became the chloroplast in plants. These complex cells,
eukaryotes, were big and energy-rich.

With energy to spare, cells began sticking together, signalling to each other, and dividing up
the labour, some feeding, some reproducing. This produced the first true multicellular
organisms. The earliest big ones we see are the Ediacaran organisms, soft quilted shapes from
around 600 million years ago, with no mouths or eyes, resting on the seafloor before the
explosion of animal life that followed.
