Life on Earth started with chemistry. In the 1950s the Miller–Urey experiment showed that
amino acids, the building blocks of proteins, could form from nothing but gas, water and
electric sparks. It didn't make life, but it made the ingredients. The real threshold came
when some molecule, probably RNA, first managed to copy itself. RNA is useful because it can
both carry information and speed up reactions, so it could be gene and enzyme at once. Once
copying existed, copying mistakes existed too, and the variants that copied best won out. That
is where natural selection begins.

For more than a billion years life stayed tiny and simple, single cells without a nucleus.
Then cyanobacteria invented photosynthesis, using sunlight to turn carbon dioxide and water
into sugar. This gave life an almost unlimited energy source. But it had a waste product:
oxygen. Over hundreds of millions of years oxygen built up, first rusting the iron dissolved
in the oceans into banded rock formations, then spilling into the air. This Great Oxidation
Event, around 2.4 billion years ago, was a catastrophe for most life of the time, because
oxygen was poisonous to them, and it may have triggered a global ice age.

Oxygen was also fuel, though, releasing far more energy than fermentation. Eventually one
larger cell engulfed an oxygen-using bacterium and kept it alive inside instead of digesting
it. That permanent partnership became the mitochondria that power our cells today. We know
this because mitochondria still carry their own DNA. The same thing happened separately with a
photosynthetic bacterium, which became the chloroplast in plants. These complex cells,
eukaryotes, were big and energy-rich.

With energy to spare, cells began sticking together, signalling to each other, and dividing
up the labour, some feeding, some reproducing. This produced the first true multicellular
organisms. The earliest big ones we see are the Ediacaran organisms, soft quilted shapes from
around 600 million years ago, with no mouths or eyes, resting on the seafloor before the
explosion of animal life that followed.
