Life on Earth started with chemistry. The Miller–Urey experiment at the University of Chicago
in the 1950s showed amino acids could form from gas, water and electric sparks. Most accounts
say life then began with a self-copying RNA molecule, but I find the metabolism-first picture
more convincing: self-sustaining chemical cycles getting going at deep-sea hydrothermal vents,
with replication coming later. Either way, once heritable copying with errors existed, natural
selection could start.

For more than a billion years life was single-celled and lacked a nucleus. Cyanobacteria then
evolved oxygenic photosynthesis, turning sunlight, water and carbon dioxide into sugar and
releasing oxygen. This gave life a vast energy source.

I don't think the rise of oxygen was really a single sudden event. There were probably local
"whiffs" of oxygen well before it accumulated for good, and the build-up was drawn out, with
oxygen first rusting dissolved iron into banded iron formations before it filled the air around
2.4 billion years ago. Whatever the pace, it was poisonous to most existing life and is linked
to severe glaciation.

Oxygen was also fuel. A larger cell engulfed an oxygen-using bacterium and kept it as a
permanent tenant; that became the mitochondrion, which still carries its own DNA. A separate
engulfment of a cyanobacterium became the chloroplast. These eukaryotes were large and
energy-rich, and with energy to spare cells began to adhere, signal and specialise, producing
the first multicellular organisms.

The earliest big ones are the Ediacaran organisms from around 600 million years ago, soft
quilted forms with no mouths or eyes. Personally I doubt most of them were true animals at all;
they look to me like a separate early experiment in multicellular life that mostly died out
before the Cambrian.
