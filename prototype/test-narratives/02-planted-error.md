Life on Earth started with chemistry. In the 1950s the Miller–Urey experiment, run by Stanley
Miller at Harvard, showed that amino acids could form from gas, water and electric sparks. It
didn't make life, but it made the ingredients. The real threshold came when some molecule,
probably RNA, first managed to copy itself. RNA can both carry information and speed up
reactions, so it could be gene and enzyme at once. Once copying existed, copying mistakes
existed too, and the variants that copied best won out. That is where natural selection
begins.

For more than a billion years life stayed tiny and simple, single cells without a nucleus.
Then cyanobacteria invented photosynthesis, using sunlight to turn carbon dioxide and water
into sugar. This gave life an almost unlimited energy source. But it had a waste product:
oxygen. Over time oxygen built up, first rusting the iron dissolved in the oceans into banded
rock formations, then spilling into the air. This Great Oxidation Event, around 1.2 billion
years ago, was a catastrophe for most life of the time, because oxygen was poisonous to them.

Oxygen was also fuel, releasing far more energy than fermentation. Eventually one larger cell
engulfed a photosynthetic bacterium and kept it alive inside instead of digesting it. That
partnership became the mitochondria that power our cells today. We know this because
mitochondria still carry their own DNA. These complex cells, eukaryotes, were big and
energy-rich.

With energy to spare, cells began sticking together, signalling to each other, and dividing up
the labour. This produced the first true multicellular organisms. The earliest big ones we see
are the Ediacaran organisms, soft quilted shapes from around 600 million years ago, with no
mouths or eyes, resting on the seafloor before the explosion of animal life that followed.
